Missing Migrants


Only last year about 3,370 people lost their lives on a migratory journey. At least other 2,512 went missing.

The US-Mexico border crossing was the deadliest one, with a record estimate of at least 715 deaths, according to the International Organization for Migration (IOM), the worst count since this agency began recording in 2014.



Each dot represents a minimum estimate of human beings dead or missing, including
refugees and asylum-seekers, in the process towards an international destination.

“The rising migrant death toll in the region is highly alarming,” said last December Michele Klein Solomon, IOM Regional Director for Central and North America and the Caribbean, in a statement.

The second deadliest path was once again the Central Mediterranean route, which connects Sub-Saharan Africa to Italy, with about 600 dead and 960 missing.

Migrants’ deaths and disappearances, and the searches that they entail, impact men and women differently.

Since most of them are men it is often the women (wives, sisters and mothers) who face not only the emotional trauma of not knowing their fate, but also plenty of legal and social challenges because of their ambiguous marital status, concluded a recent study by IOM on Ethiopian migrants.

Overall more than 32,200 people have lost their lives since 2014, while IOM estimates that roughly 25,000 have gone missing.
